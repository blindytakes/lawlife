What to Do After a Car Accident in South Carolina

CK
Chase Keibler
Founding Attorney
March 15, 2026
8 min read

The moments after a car accident in South Carolina can be overwhelming. Your heart is racing, adrenaline is pumping, and you may be in pain. But what you do — and don't do — in the minutes, hours, and days following a crash can make or break your ability to recover fair compensation.

As a trial attorney who has represented both injured individuals and insurance companies, I've seen firsthand how early mistakes can derail an otherwise strong case. Here's what South Carolina law says you should do, and what I tell every client who calls me after an accident.

Step 1: Check for Injuries and Call 911

Your first priority is safety. Check yourself and your passengers for injuries. If anyone is hurt — even if it seems minor — call 911 immediately. Under South Carolina law (S.C. Code Ann. 56-5-1210), you are required to remain at the scene of any accident involving injury, death, or significant property damage.

Even if you feel fine, keep in mind that adrenaline can mask serious injuries. Traumatic brain injuries, soft tissue damage, and internal bleeding often don't present symptoms until hours or even days later.

Important: Don't refuse medical attention

If EMTs arrive, let them evaluate you. Refusing medical treatment at the scene is one of the first things insurance companies use to argue your injuries aren't serious. Even if you feel okay, get checked out.

Step 2: Move to Safety (If You Can)

If your vehicle is drivable and it's safe to do so, move it out of the flow of traffic. South Carolina's "move over" law encourages drivers to clear the roadway to prevent secondary accidents. Turn on your hazard lights. If you can't move your car, stay inside with your seatbelt on until help arrives.

Step 3: Document Everything

Once you're safe, start collecting evidence. This is critical. The more documentation you have, the stronger your case will be. Here's what to capture:

  • Photos of the scene: Damage to all vehicles, skid marks, traffic signs, road conditions, weather, and the overall scene from multiple angles
  • Photos of injuries: Even minor cuts, bruises, or swelling. Photograph them again over the following days as they develop
  • Other driver's information: Name, phone, insurance company and policy number, driver's license number, license plate
  • Witness information: Names and phone numbers of anyone who saw the accident
  • The police report number: If officers respond, get their names, badge numbers, and the report number

"In my experience, cases with strong photo documentation from the scene settle for significantly more than those without. Your phone is your best friend in the moments after an accident."

Step 4: Don't Admit Fault — To Anyone

This is where I see people make costly mistakes every week. In the chaos after an accident, it's natural to say things like "I'm sorry" or "I didn't see you." Don't. Even an innocent apology can be used against you by the other driver's insurance company.

Stick to the facts when speaking with the other driver and police officers. "The light was green when I entered the intersection" is factual. "I'm so sorry, I should have been paying more attention" is an admission that can cost you tens of thousands of dollars.

Step 5: Get Medical Treatment Within 72 Hours

Even if you went to the emergency room at the scene, follow up with your primary care doctor or an urgent care facility within 72 hours. This creates a documented medical record linking your injuries to the accident — something insurance companies look for immediately.

Common delayed-onset injuries from car accidents include:

  • Whiplash — neck pain, stiffness, headaches
  • Concussion / TBI — confusion, dizziness, light sensitivity
  • Herniated discs — back pain, numbness, shooting pain down legs
  • Internal bleeding — abdominal pain, dizziness, fainting

Step 6: Don't Talk to the Other Driver's Insurance Company

Within days of the accident, you'll likely get a call from the at-fault driver's insurance adjuster. They'll be friendly. They'll say they just want to "get your side of the story." They may even offer a quick settlement.

Do not give a recorded statement. Do not accept their first offer.

Insurance adjusters are trained to minimize payouts. That friendly phone call is a strategy. Anything you say can and will be used to reduce your claim. The initial settlement offer is almost always a fraction of what your case is actually worth.

How low do they go?

We recently had a client with a traumatic brain injury where the insurance company's initial offer was $45,000. After we took the case, we recovered nearly $1 million. That's not unusual — it's the norm when you have experienced representation.

Step 7: Contact a Personal Injury Attorney

South Carolina has a three-year statute of limitations for personal injury claims (S.C. Code Ann. 15-3-530). But don't wait. Evidence disappears, witnesses forget, and insurance companies build their defense from day one.

A good personal injury attorney will:

  • Investigate the accident scene and preserve evidence
  • Handle all communication with insurance companies
  • Calculate the full value of your claim (medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, future care)
  • Negotiate aggressively — and take the case to trial if necessary

At Keibler Law Group, consultations are always free, and we work on a contingency fee basis. That means you pay nothing unless we win your case.

What If the Accident Wasn't Entirely My Fault?

South Carolina follows a modified comparative negligence rule. This means you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault — as long as your share of fault is less than 51%. However, your compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault.

For example, if you're found 20% at fault and your damages total $100,000, you'd recover $80,000. This is another reason why proper documentation and legal representation matter — the other side will always try to shift blame onto you.
