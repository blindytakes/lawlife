5 Things Insurance Companies Don't Want You to Know

CK
Chase Keibler
Founding Attorney
February 28, 2026
5 min read

After an injury, you'd think your insurance company would have your back. You've been paying premiums for years, after all. But here's the reality: insurance companies are publicly traded corporations with a fiduciary duty to their shareholders — not to you. Their entire business model depends on collecting more in premiums than they pay out in claims.

Having spent years representing both injured individuals and insurance companies, I've seen the playbook from both sides. Here are five things the insurance industry doesn't want you to know.

1. The First Offer Is Almost Never Fair

Within days of your accident, an adjuster may call with a settlement offer. It might sound generous — especially when you're stressed about medical bills and lost wages. It almost certainly isn't.

Insurance companies know that injured people are vulnerable early on. They're counting on you accepting a quick check before you understand the full extent of your injuries or the true value of your claim. We've seen initial offers that were less than 10% of what the case was actually worth.

Real example

A client came to us after being offered $45,000 for a traumatic brain injury from a car accident. After we took the case, we recovered nearly $1 million. The insurance company knew the case was worth more — they were betting our client didn't.

2. That "Friendly" Adjuster Is Not Your Friend

Insurance adjusters are trained professionals. Their job performance is measured by how much money they save the company — not by how fairly they treat you. When they call to "check in" or "get your side of the story," they're building a case against you.

Common tactics include:

  • Recorded statements: Asking you to describe the accident on record, then using any inconsistency against you later
  • Medical authorizations: Getting you to sign broad releases so they can comb through your entire medical history looking for pre-existing conditions
  • Surveillance: Yes, they hire investigators. Social media monitoring is standard practice
  • Delay tactics: Dragging out the process hoping you'll get desperate and settle for less

3. You Don't Have to Accept Their Damage Valuation

When the insurance company tells you what your car is "worth" or what your medical bills should have been, that's their opinion — not a legal fact. They routinely use software programs that systematically undervalue claims.

You have the right to get your own estimates, hire your own experts, and negotiate. If they're lowballing your property damage, they're almost certainly lowballing your injury claim too.

4. Your Own Insurance Company Can Work Against You

Here's one that surprises people: even your own insurance company's interests aren't always aligned with yours. In underinsured motorist claims or first-party claims, your insurer is the one paying out — which means they have every incentive to minimize what they owe you.

South Carolina's bad faith insurance laws (S.C. Code Ann. 38-59-20) do provide some protection. If your insurer unreasonably refuses to pay a valid claim, you may be entitled to additional damages. But you need an attorney who knows how to hold them accountable.

"The insurance company is never going to tell you that you're entitled to more money. That's not how the game works. You need someone in your corner who knows the rules."

5. Having a Lawyer Usually Means a Bigger Settlement — Even After Fees

Insurance companies love when injured people try to handle claims on their own. Why? Because unrepresented claimants settle for significantly less. Studies consistently show that people with attorneys recover 3 to 5 times more than those without — even after attorney fees are deducted.

Think about it this way: if the insurance company thought hiring a lawyer wouldn't affect your payout, they wouldn't spend so much effort discouraging you from getting one.
